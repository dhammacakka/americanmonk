To perfectly realign the lunar calendar with the solar seasons, the calendar operates on a **19-year cycle**, during which it adds exactly **seven extra lunar months**. Since an extra lunar month is 30 days long, adding seven of them equals 210 days—which almost perfectly erases the 209 days of drift!
How do we fit 7 leap months into 19 years? We adjust for this time lag at intervals of either three or two years. The pattern looks like this:
* 3 years, 3 years, 2 years (An 8-year span)
* 3 years, 3 years, 2 years (Another 8-year span)
* 3 years (The final span)
8 + 8 + 3 = 19 years.
Therefore, in the Lunar Calendar, we add an extra lunar month (two *pakkha*) to the Hot Season. While a season typically comprises eight *pakkha* (new or full moon cycles), this year's Hot Season includes ten.

**The 30-Day Fine-Tuning**
Because the universe is rarely mathematically perfect, there is one final adjustment. To fix fractions of a day that build up over decades, the calendar occasionally designates a "Great Leap Year." In these specific years, an extra leap day is added to the third month (Nayon), extending it from its normal 29 days to 30 days to keep everything flawlessly synchronized.
